How to sell your house without an estate agent in Melbourne

Everything an agent does, you can do — most of it in evenings and weekends. Here's the whole process, in order, in plain English.

1. Yes, it's legal — here's the one thing you must do first

There's no law that says an estate agent has to be involved in selling your home. What the law does require is an Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) before your home is marketed. If your home has been sold or let in the last ten years it probably already has one — check the national EPC register online. If not, book an accredited domestic energy assessor: expect £60–£90 and a visit of under an hour. Sorted? You're legally ready to sell.

2. Price it like a buyer, not an owner

The single biggest factor in whether your home sells is the asking price. Three checks before you commit to a number:

  • Sold prices, not asking prices. HM Land Registry publishes the actual price every home sold for. Search your street and the streets around it for the last two years.
  • What's on the market now in DE73. Your home will be compared with these, so be honest about how yours stacks up on condition, parking and garden.
  • Melbourne is its own market. A period home near the Pool or Market Place behaves differently from a modern estate house — compare like with like.

A realistic price from day one beats "start high, reduce later" — reductions are visible and make buyers wonder what's wrong.

3. Take photos that do your home justice

You don't need a professional (though there's one on our services page if you'd like one). You need: daylight, tidy rooms, landscape orientation, and the camera held at chest height. Open the curtains, turn the lights on, hide the bins, and take the front-of-house shot from across the road in morning light. One great photo is free with every listing; up to ten with Boost.

4. Write the description only you could write

Agents write "deceptively spacious". You can write "the kitchen gets the sun from 2pm and you can walk to the Pool in four minutes". Cover the facts — rooms, tenure, parking, garden, EPC rating, council tax band — then add the two or three things you'd genuinely miss. 100–200 words is plenty. Be accurate: a buyer is entitled to the material facts, and honesty up front prevents fall-throughs later.

5. Handle enquiries like a pro

When a buyer calls, take their name and number, then ask the two questions every agent asks: "What's your position?" (first-time buyer, sold subject to contract, still to sell, cash?) and "Have you got a mortgage agreement in principle?" You're not being nosy — you're qualifying. Prioritise viewings for proceedable buyers.

6. Run viewings safely and well

  • Have a second adult in the house, or at least tell someone the viewing time and the buyer's name and number.
  • Daylight viewings where possible; 20–30 minutes is normal.
  • Lead the tour, end in the best room, then let them wander back through alone — buyers talk honestly when you're not in the room.
  • Don't negotiate on the doorstep. "Have a think and give me a ring" is a complete sentence.

7. Offers: the part people fear, simplified

An offer is just a number and a position. Before accepting, ask for proof of funds — a mortgage agreement in principle plus deposit evidence, or bank statements for a cash buyer. It's normal and any serious buyer expects it. Negotiate by phone, confirm by text or email so there's a written record, and remember nothing is binding in England until exchange of contracts — for either side.

8. Hand it to the professionals

Once you accept, email both solicitors the same short memo: property address, agreed price, buyer's full name and contact details, seller's full name and contact details, and each side's solicitor. That's the "memorandum of sale" agents charge thousands to send. From here your conveyancer runs the legal process exactly as they would in any sale — searches, enquiries, exchange, completion. Typical timeline: 8–14 weeks.

9. What to expect, honestly

Private selling costs you a few hours of admin and saves you thousands of pounds. The trade-off is that you answer the phone and host the viewings. In a town like Melbourne — where many buyers are local, know the streets and may even know your house — that's usually an advantage, not a chore.

A note on this guide. This is general information, not legal or financial advice. Your conveyancer is the right person for legal questions about your sale.
